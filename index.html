Don’t Stop Me Now: Evaluating Matched Realities for In-Car XR User Experience

Abstract

Understanding how visual-physical alignment influences user experience is critical for the deployment of extended reality (XR) systems in automotive contexts. While Mixed Reality (MR) and Virtual Reality (VR) are often assumed to differ in realism and comfort, it remains unclear how these differences manifest when virtual driving is grounded in real vehicle controls. We present a physically grounded XR driving platform that integrates real vehicle inputs via OBD-II telemetry with immersive virtual environments. We evaluated the system with 40 participants performing a controlled driving task under both MR and VR conditions. Subjective measures included perceived realism, visuo-tactile coherence, control quality, immersion, and cybersickness. Contrary to common expectations, results show largely comparable experiences between MR and VR across most measures, including immersion, visuo-tactile perception, and discomfort. Only modest trends suggest slightly higher perceived driving realism and control responsiveness in MR. These findings indicate that grounding XR simulations in real vehicle interaction may reduce experiential differences between XR modalities. We discuss implications for the design of XR-based automotive simulators, suggesting that physical integration may play a more critical role than display modality in shaping user experience.
